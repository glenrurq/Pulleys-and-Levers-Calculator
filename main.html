Introduction

Pulleys and levers have been essential tools throughout human history, accompanying progress and great achievements since ancient times. These simple machines have enabled entire civilizations to build impressive monuments, navigators to cross seas, and peoples to transform their environment with ingenuity and effort.
The famous phrase attributed to Archimedes, "Give me a fulcrum and I will move the world," encapsulates the power that the lever has had as a symbol of human ingenuity. It was not only a physical tool, but also a driving force that helped us understand that with the right combination of strength and strategy, the impossible can become possible.
Throughout history, pulleys have allowed men and women to lift great loads and overcome seemingly insurmountable challenges, playing silent protagonists in the construction of architectural marvels and in the development of navigation and commerce. Without a doubt, pulleys and levers have been fundamental allies in transforming our environment and shaping the world we know today. His legacy is not only technical, but also a lesson in how human creativity can change the course of history with simple yet powerful tools.

How it works?

A pulley is a wheel with a groove through which a rope or cable passes. Pulling on one end of the rope transmits a motion that allows you to lift an object attached to the other end. What makes a pulley interesting is that, depending on how it's configured, it can help us change the direction of the force we apply, reduce the amount of force needed, or both at the same time.
There are three main types of pulleys: the fixed pulley, the mobile pulley, and the compound pulley (also called a block and tackle). Each one has a different mechanical advantage and, therefore, a different force formula.

Fixed Pulley: It's anchored at one point and simply changes the direction of the force. For example, instead of having to lift a load by pulling upward, we can pull downward, which is more comfortable. However, the force needed is not reduced. Its mechanical advantage is 1, so:
Force = Resistance
F = R

Movable Pulley: This pulley moves with the load. It splits the weight between two sections of rope, so only half the force is needed to lift the object. Its mechanical advantage is 2:
Force = Resistance / 2
F = R / 2

Compound Pulley (Block and Tackle): This is a system that combines multiple pulleys—half fixed and half movable. The more movable pulleys you add, the greater the mechanical advantage. The general rule is that each movable pulley doubles the advantage, so the total mechanical advantage is 2n, where n is the number of movable pulleys. The formula becomes:
Force = Resistance / 2ⁿ
F = R / 2ⁿ
For example, if there are 3 movable pulleys, the mechanical advantage is 8, and the required force is only one-eighth of the load. These systems are commonly used in cranes and hoists to lift very heavy objects with minimal effort.

Let's now turn to the lever, another fundamental simple machine. A lever is a rigid bar that rotates about a fulcrum. With a lever, we can move or lift a load by applying a force in the right place. This mechanism was first described by the Greek mathematician Archimedes, who said, "Give me a fulcrum, and I will move the world." This phrase nicely sums up the power of a well-used lever.
A lever has three main elements: the force, which is the force we apply; the resistance, which is the weight or load we want to move; and the fulcrum, which is the point around which the bar pivots. Depending on the position of these three elements, there are three types or degrees of lever: first degree, second degree, and third degree.

First-Degree Lever: In a first-degree lever, the fulcrum is between the force and the resistance. A typical example of this type of lever is a seesaw or a pair of scissors. In this case, the direction of the force can be changed. For example, by applying a downward force, we can lift an object upward. This type of lever also allows the required force to be reduced if the power arm is longer than the resistance arm. The general formula we use for all levers is:
Power × power arm = Resistance × resistance arm
When the arm where we apply the force is longer than the load arm, we need less force. In other words, by increasing the distance, we reduce the effort. This is the key to levers. In the first-degree lever, if the arms are well distributed, a balance or mechanical advantage can be achieved that reduces the force required.

Second-Degree Lever: The resistance is in the middle, between the fulcrum and the power. That is, first comes the fulcrum, then the load, and finally the point where we apply the force. A classic example is the wheelbarrow. This type of lever always reduces the force required, since the power arm is longer than the resistance arm. Although it doesn't change the direction of movement, it is very useful for transporting heavy loads with little effort.
Power × power arm = Resistance × resistance arm
In this type of lever, the longer power arm ensures that the force we need to apply is less than the weight of the load. This is why a person can lift a heavy load with a wheelbarrow, even if they are not very strong.

Third-Degree Lever: This is the least intuitive. In it, the power is located between the fulcrum and the resistance. An example of this is the human arm when we lift an object with our hand. The elbow acts as the fulcrum, the muscle applies the force (power) in the middle of the arm, and the resistance is in the hand. This type of lever does not reduce the force required, but rather increases it. However, it has the advantage of allowing for faster and more precise movements.
Power × power arm = Resistance × resistance arm
Although in this case the force applied may be greater than the weight of the load, we gain speed and precision. This is why it's common for tools or body parts where fine control of movement is required.
